Description

The ITDT ontology is an extension of RiC-O (https://www.ica.org/standards/RiC/ontology) created to describe the architectural records of the Italian architect and engineer Dino Tamburini. The ontology provides sub-classes of the class rico:Record for description of architectural graphical documents. The main concept of the ontology is the Architectural Project, which is seen from three different perspectives: that of file, that of knowledge process and that of potential built work.

Overview

ITDT Ontology is named after the short name of the archive it was used to describe (ITaly Dino Tamburini). The ontology extends classes and properties of Records In Contexts Ontology by International Council on Archives to provide means for semantic description of typical records found in architectural archives and architectural projects. If you are familiar with RiC-O or RiC-CM, the Fig.1 summarizes the extension. This documentation includes both ITDT entities and imported RiC-O entities.

Figure 1: Implemented sub-classes and sub-properties of RiC-O.

The class Architectural Record extends generic class rico:Record and it is superclass for classes that aggregate architectural records based on their content type, such as Working Drawing, Architectural Photograph or Physical Model

Instantiationc

URI https://www.ica.org/standards/RiC/ontology#Instantiation
Is Defined By https://www.ica.org/standards/RiC/ontology
Description

The inscription of information made by an Agent on a physical carrier in any persistent, recoverable form as a means of communicating information through time and space.
Usage Note A Record or Record Part must have been instantiated at least once, though this instantiation may no longer exist at the moment of description. An instantiation might also exist at the moment of description, but be destroyed at a later moment in time, when, for example, a derived instantiation might become the only remaining instantiation. A Record Set may have an instantiation, which is to say that it is not a necessary condition. An Instantiation may be derived from another Instantiation. A Record Resource may have many Instantiations simultaneously (for instance, a record printed and saved in the same time as DOCX and PDF/A would have 3 concurrent instantiations) or through time (for example, copy of a record). Depending on the context, a new instantiation may be seen as a new or as the same record resource. During in the process of re-instantiation something is lost and something is preserved, but it is up to the context and the Agent that produces or uses that Instantiation to assess whether the two instantiations are functionally equivalent or not. Successive instantiations may change the perceivable boundaries of a Record Resource. For instance, a case file comprising many records may be digitized and saved as one single PDF file, which, from management perspective, may be treated as one Record. Similarly, a large Record Set (a fonds or a series) may be maintained as one database. On the other hand, one record (main document and its annexes) may be digitized in separate files and each one may be managed as a discrete â€œphysicalâ€ item.
